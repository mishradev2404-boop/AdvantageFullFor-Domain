B2B Content Marketing for LinkedIn

LinkedIn is not a distribution channel for your blog. It rewards content built for the feed and for the way B2B buyers actually decide.

What's inside

  1. What content marketing covers
  2. How content and campaigns connect

Repurposing a blog post into a LinkedIn update is the most common content strategy in B2B and one of the least effective. The feed does not reward summaries with a link out. It rewards content that delivers its value in place — and content that maps to where the reader actually is in the buying process.

Most B2B content is written for people who have already decided to buy. That is a small fraction of your market at any given moment. The rest are either unaware they have the problem, aware but not yet looking, or comparing options. Content that only speaks to the last group leaves the other three untouched.

01
What content marketing covers

Strategic content planning

We map content to funnel stage before writing anything. Awareness pieces that frame the problem, consideration pieces that explain approaches, and decision pieces — case studies, comparisons, proof — that give a buyer confidence to choose you. Every asset has a defined job and a defined next step.

Case studies and white papers

High-value assets do double duty: they give your sales team something substantive to send, and they give your paid campaigns something worth gating. A well-built case study is frequently the highest-converting asset in a B2B account, because it answers the question buyers are actually asking — has this worked for someone like me?

Video and rich media

Video and document ads consistently earn more attention in the LinkedIn feed than static images, largely because they hold people in place rather than asking them to leave. Document ads in particular are one of the most underused formats on the platform — they preview in-feed, so readers self-qualify before they ever fill in a form.

02
How content and campaigns connect

Content that nobody sees is a cost, not an asset. Every piece we produce is built with a distribution plan attached: which audience it targets, whether it runs organically, as paid promotion, or as a gated asset behind a lead gen form, and what happens to the people who engage with it. That last part — the follow-up sequence — is where most content programmes quietly lose the pipeline they generated.

Common questions

Do you produce the design and video, or just the writing?

Both. Content built for the LinkedIn feed needs the design and the copy to work together, so we handle production as well as strategy.

Can you work with content we already have?

Usually yes. Most companies have more usable material than they realise — sales decks, webinar recordings and internal documents are often the fastest route to a strong asset.

Should our content be gated or free?

It depends on the job. Awareness content generally performs better ungated; high-value decision-stage assets are the ones worth gating. Gating everything is a common mistake.
